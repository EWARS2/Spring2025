Seed of one of several genera of the plant family Fabaceae
For other uses, see  /wiki/Bean_(disambiguation) Bean (disambiguation).
 /wiki/File:French_beans_J1.JPG  /wiki/French_bean French bean pods on a plant
A bean is the seed of any plant in the  /wiki/Legume legume family ( /wiki/Fabaceae Fabaceae) used as a  /wiki/Vegetable vegetable for human consumption or animal feed. #cite_note-1 [1] The seeds are often preserved through  /wiki/Drying drying, but fresh beans are also sold. Most beans are traditionally soaked and boiled, but they can be cooked in many different ways, #cite_note-2 [2] including frying and baking, and are used in many traditional dishes throughout the world.  The unripe seedpods of some varieties are also eaten whole as  /wiki/Green_bean green beans or  /wiki/Edamame edamame (immature  /wiki/Soybean soybean), but fully ripened beans contain toxins like  /wiki/Phytohaemagglutinin phytohemagglutinin and require cooking.

Terminology
-----

The word 'bean', for the Old World vegetable, existed in  /wiki/Old_English Old English, #cite_note-3 [3] long before the  /wiki/New_World New World genus  /wiki/Phaseolus Phaseolus was known in Europe.  With the  /wiki/Columbian_exchange Columbian exchange of domestic plants between Europe and the Americas, use of the word was extended to pod-borne seeds of Phaseolus, such as the  /wiki/Common_bean common bean and the  /wiki/Runner_bean runner bean, and the related genus  /wiki/Vigna Vigna. The term has long been applied generally to seeds of similar form, #cite_note-AHD-4 [4] such as Old World  /wiki/Soybean soybeans and  /wiki/Lupinus lupins, and to the fruits or seeds of unrelated plants such as  /wiki/Coffee_bean coffee beans,  /wiki/Vanilla vanilla beans,  /wiki/Ricinus castor beans, and  /wiki/Cocoa_bean cocoa beans. #cite_note-FAOdef-5 [5]

History
-----

See also:  /wiki/Columbian_exchange Columbian exchange
Beans in an early cultivated form were grown in Thailand from the early seventh millennium BCE, predating ceramics. #cite_note-6 [6] Beans were deposited with the dead in  /wiki/Ancient_Egypt ancient Egypt. Not until the second millennium BCE did cultivated, large-seeded broad beans appear in the  /wiki/Aegean_civilization Aegean region,  /wiki/Iberia Iberia, and transalpine Europe. #cite_note-7 [7] In the  /wiki/Iliad Iliad (8th century BCE), there is a passing mention of beans and  /wiki/Chickpea chickpeas cast on the threshing floor. #cite_note-8 [8]
The oldest-known domesticated beans in the Americas were found in  /wiki/Guitarrero_Cave Guitarrero Cave,  /wiki/Peru Peru, dated to around the second millennium BCE. #cite_note-Chazan-9 [9] Genetic analyses of the common bean Phaseolus show that it originated in  /wiki/Mesoamerica Mesoamerica, and subsequently spread southward, along with  /wiki/Maize maize and squash, traditional companion crops. #cite_note-10 [10]
Most of the kinds of beans commonly eaten today are part of the genus Phaseolus, which originated in the Americas. The first European to encounter them was  /wiki/Christopher_Columbus Christopher Columbus, while exploring what may have been the  /wiki/Bahamas Bahamas, and saw them growing in fields. Five kinds of  /wiki/Phaseolus Phaseolus beans were domesticated by pre-Columbian peoples, selecting  /wiki/Dehiscence_(botany) pods that did not open and scatter their seeds when ripe: common beans ( /wiki/Phaseolus_vulgaris P. vulgaris) grown from Chile to the northern part of the United States; lima and sieva beans ( /wiki/Phaseolus_lunatus P. lunatus); and the less widely distributed teparies ( /wiki/Phaseolus_acutifolius P. acutifolius), scarlet runner beans ( /wiki/Phaseolus_coccineus P. coccineus), and polyanthus beans. #cite_note-FOOTNOTEKaplan200830-11 [11]
 /wiki/Pre-Columbian_era Pre-Columbian peoples as far north as the Atlantic seaboard grew beans in the " /wiki/Three_Sisters_(agriculture) Three Sisters" method of  /wiki/Companion_planting companion planting. The beans were interplanted with  /wiki/Maize maize and  /wiki/Cucurbita squash. #cite_note-Mt._Pleasant_2006-12 [12] Beans were cultivated across Chile in Pre-Hispanic times, likely as far south as the  /wiki/Chilo%C3%A9_Archipelago ChiloÃ© Archipelago. #cite_note-Alimentarias-13 [13]

Conservation of cultivars
-----

The  /wiki/Biodiversity biodiversity of bean cultivars is threatened by modern plant breeding, which selects a small number of the most productive varieties. Efforts are being made to conserve the  /wiki/Germplasm germplasm of older varieties in different countries. #cite_note-24 [24] #cite_note-25 [25] As of 2023, the Norwegian  /wiki/Svalbard_Global_Seed_Vault Svalbard Global Seed Vault holds more than 40,000  /wiki/Accession_number_(cultural_property) accessions of Phaseolus bean species. #cite_note-26 [26]

Agronomy
-----

Many beans are summer crops that needs warm temperatures to grow; peas are an exception. Legumes are capable of  /wiki/Nitrogen_fixation nitrogen fixation and hence need less fertiliser than most plants. Maturity is typically 55â€“60 days from planting to harvest. #cite_note-27 [27] As the pods mature, they turn yellow and dry up, and the beans inside change from green to their mature colour. Many beans are  /wiki/Vine vines needing external support, such as "bean cages" or poles. Native Americans customarily grew them along with corn and squash, the tall stalks acting as support for the beans. #cite_note-28 [28]
More recently, the commercial "bush bean" which does not require support and produces all its pods simultaneously has been developed. #cite_note-29 [29]

Uses
-----


Culinary
-----

Beans can be cooked in a wide variety of  /wiki/Casserole casseroles,  /wiki/Curries curries,  /wiki/Salad salads,  /wiki/Soup soups, and  /wiki/Stew stews. They can be served whole or mashed alongside meat or toast, or included in an  /wiki/Omelette omelette or a  /wiki/Flatbread flatbread wrap. #cite_note-BBC_Good_Food-36 [36] Other options are to include them in a bake with a  /wiki/Cheese_sauce cheese sauce, a Mexican-style  /wiki/Chili_con_carne chili con carne, or to use them as a meat substitute in a  /wiki/Hamburger burger or in  /wiki/Falafel falafels. #cite_note-37 [37] The French  /wiki/Cassoulet cassoulet is a slow-cooked stew with haricot beans, sausage, pork, mutton, and preserved goose. #cite_note-38 [38] Soybeans can be processed into bean curd ( /wiki/Tofu tofu) #cite_note-39 [39] or fermented into a cake ( /wiki/Tempeh tempeh); #cite_note-40 [40] these can be eaten fried or roasted like meat, or included in stir-fries, curries, and soups. #cite_note-41 [41] #cite_note-42 [42] #cite_note-43 [43] Most dry beans contain 21-25%  /wiki/Protein protein by weight; #cite_note-44 [44] dry  /wiki/Soybean soybeans are 36.5% protein by weight. #cite_note-45 [45]

Health concerns
-----


Toxins
-----

Main articles:  /wiki/Phytohaemagglutinin Phytohaemagglutinin and  /wiki/Soybean_agglutinin Soybean agglutinin
Some kinds of raw beans contain a harmful, flavourless toxin: the  /wiki/Lectin lectin  /wiki/Phytohaemagglutinin phytohaemagglutinin, which must be destroyed by cooking. Red  /wiki/Kidney_bean kidney beans are particularly toxic, but other types also pose risks of  /wiki/Food_poisoning food poisoning. Even small quantities (4 or 5 raw beans) may cause severe stomachache, vomiting, and diarrhea. This risk does not apply to canned beans because they have already been cooked. #cite_note-47 [47] A recommended method is to boil the beans for at least ten minutes; under-cooked beans may be more toxic than raw beans. #cite_note-FDA-48 [48]
Cooking beans, without bringing them to a boil, in a  /wiki/Slow_cooker slow cooker at a temperature well below boiling may not destroy toxins. #cite_note-FDA-48 [48] A case of poisoning by  /wiki/Lima_bean butter beans used to make  /wiki/Falafel falafel was reported; the beans were used instead of traditional  /wiki/Broad_bean broad beans or  /wiki/Chickpea chickpeas, soaked and ground without boiling, made into patties, and  /wiki/Shallow_frying shallow fried. #cite_note-falafel-49 [49]
Bean poisoning is not well known in the medical community, and many cases may be misdiagnosed or never reported; figures appear not to be available. In the case of the UK  /wiki/National_Poisons_Information_Service National Poisons Information Service, available only to health professionals, the dangers of beans other than red beans were not flagged as of 2008 https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Bean&action=edit [update]. #cite_note-falafel-49 [49]
 /wiki/Fermentation_(food) Fermentation is used in some parts of Africa to improve the nutritional value of beans by removing toxins. Inexpensive fermentation improves the nutritional impact of flour from dry beans and improves digestibility, according to research co-authored by Emire Shimelis, from the Food Engineering Program at Addis Ababa University. #cite_note-50 [50] Beans are a major source of dietary  /wiki/Protein_(nutrient) protein in Kenya, Malawi, Tanzania, Uganda and Zambia. #cite_note-51 [51]

Other hazards
-----

It is common to make  /wiki/Beansprout beansprouts by letting some types of bean, often  /wiki/Mung_bean mung beans, germinate in moist and warm conditions; beansprouts may be used as ingredients in cooked dishes, or eaten raw or lightly cooked. There have been many  /wiki/Beansprout#health_concerns outbreaks of disease from bacterial contamination, often by  /wiki/Salmonella salmonella,  /wiki/Listeria listeria, and  /wiki/Escherichia_coli Escherichia coli,  of beansprouts not thoroughly cooked, #cite_note-52 [52] some causing significant mortality. #cite_note-53 [53]
Many types of bean like kidney bean contain significant amounts of  /wiki/Antinutrient antinutrients that inhibit some enzyme processes in the body.  /wiki/Phytic_acid Phytic acid, present in beans, interferes with bone growth and interrupts  /wiki/Vitamin_D vitamin D metabolism. #cite_note-54 [54] #cite_note-55 [55]
Many beans, including broad beans, navy beans, kidney beans and soybeans, contain large sugar molecules,  /wiki/Oligosaccharide oligosaccharides (particularly  /wiki/Raffinose raffinose and  /wiki/Stachyose stachyose). A suitable oligosaccharide-cleaving  /wiki/Enzyme enzyme is necessary to digest these. As the human digestive tract does not contain such enzymes, consumed oligosaccharides are digested by  /wiki/Bacteria bacteria in the large intestine, producing gases such as methane, released as  /wiki/Flatulence flatulence. #cite_note-56 [56] #cite_note-57 [57] #cite_note-58 [58] #cite_note-59 [59]

In human society
-----
 /wiki/File:Carracci_-_Der_Bohnenesser.jpeg  /wiki/The_Beaneater The Beaneater (c.1584) by  /wiki/Annibale_Carracci Annibale Carracci 
Beans have often been thought of as a food of the poor, as small farmers ate grains, vegetables, and got their protein from beans, while the wealthier classes were able to afford meat. #cite_note-Albala_2007-60 [60] European society has what Ken Albala calls "a class-based antagonism" to beans. #cite_note-Albala_2007-60 [60] 
Different cultures agree in disliking the flatulence that beans cause, and possess their own seasonings to attempt to remedy it: Mexico uses the herb  /wiki/Epazote epazote; India the aromatic resin  /wiki/Asafoetida asafoetida; Germany applies the herb  /wiki/Satureja savory; in the Middle East,  /wiki/Cumin cumin; and Japan the seaweed  /wiki/Kombu kombu. #cite_note-Albala_2007-60 [60] A substance for which there is evidence of effectiveness in reducing flatulence is the enzyme  /wiki/Alpha-galactosidase alpha-galactosidase; #cite_note-Albala_2007-60 [60] extracted from the  /wiki/Mould mould fungus  /wiki/Aspergillus_niger Aspergillus niger, it breaks down  /wiki/Glycolipid glycolipids and  /wiki/Glycoprotein glycoproteins. #cite_note-61 [61] #cite_note-62 [62] The reputation of beans for flatulence is the theme of a children's song " /wiki/Beans,_Beans,_the_Musical_Fruit Beans, Beans, the Musical Fruit". #cite_note-63 [63]
The  /wiki/Mexican_jumping_bean Mexican jumping bean is a segment of a seed pod occupied by the  /wiki/Larva larva of the moth  /wiki/Cydia_saltitans Cydia saltitans, and sold as a novelty. The pods start to jump when warmed in the palm of the hand. Scientists have suggested that the random walk that results may help the larva to find shade and so to survive on hot days. #cite_note-64 [64]
